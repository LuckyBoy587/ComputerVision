Task 4: Analysis¶

1. Which trajectory converges smoothest?¶

Experiment C (using learning rate decay) converges the smoothest. Although Experiment A ($\alpha = 0.05$) converges, it exhibits oscillations in the beginning along the $w_2$ direction. Experiment C reduces the learning rate step-by-step, allowing rapid progress initially and then suppressing oscillations as it approaches the minimum, leading to a smooth path to the optimum.

2. What happens to $w_2$ under Experiment B? Why?¶

Under Experiment B ($\alpha = 0.09$), the trajectory for $w_2$ oscillates wildly back and forth across the valley.

  • The gradient along $w_2$ is $\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_2} = 20 w_2$.
  • The update equation is $w_2^{(t+1)} = w_2^{(t)} - \alpha (20 w_2^{(t)}) = w_2^{(t)}(1 - 20\alpha)$.
  • For $\alpha = 0.09$, the scaling factor is $1 - 20(0.09) = 1 - 1.8 = -0.8$.
  • Since the factor is negative and has an absolute value close to $1.0$ ($|-0.8| < 1.0$), $w_2$ alternates signs and oscillates heavily across the valley, but still slowly decays toward $0.0$. If $\alpha \ge 0.1$, the updates would diverge or oscillate indefinitely, leading to complete instability along the $w_2$ axis.

3. How does learning rate decay help on ill-conditioned surfaces?¶

An ill-conditioned surface (like this elongated bowl) has a much larger gradient along one axis ($w_2$ with weight 10) than the other ($w_1$ with weight 1). A constant high learning rate suited for $w_1$ causes instability and wild oscillations along $w_2$. A constant low learning rate avoids oscillations along $w_2$ but makes progress along $w_1$ extremely slow. Learning rate decay solves this by dynamically decreasing the learning rate over time:

  • In early steps, a larger learning rate allows fast progress along the flatter $w_1$ direction.
  • As the learning rate decays, it becomes small enough to prevent/dampen oscillations along the steep $w_2$ direction, allowing the optimizer to safely settle into the minimum.
